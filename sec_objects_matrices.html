Section 2.3 Matrices

¶
Definition 2.3.1.

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers. A matrix with \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns is called an \(m \times n\) matrix.

A matrix with the same number of rows and columns is called a square matrix.

We can represent a matrix in the following way:

  • \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \dots & a_{1n} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \dots & a_{mn} \\ \end{bmatrix} \)
  • The \(i\)th row is \(\begin{bmatrix} a_{i1} & a_{i2} & \dots & a_{in} \\ \end{bmatrix} \text{,}\) a \(1\times n\) row matrix.
  • The \(j\)th column is \(\begin{bmatrix} a_{1j} \\ a_{2j} \\ \vdots \\ a_{mj} \\ \end{bmatrix} \text{,}\) an \(m\times 1\) column matrix.
  • The \(ij\)th term of the matrix is denoted \(a_{ij}\)
  • We will often write \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{ij} \end{bmatrix} \) and give a rule for \(a_{ij}\) in order to conserve space when relevant.
Example 2.3.2.

For the given matrix, answer the following questions:

\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 2 & -6 & 0 & 1 \\ 3 & -1 & 4 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
  1. What are the dimensions of \(A\text{?}\)
  2. What is the third column of \(A\text{?}\)
  3. What is the second row of \(A\text{?}\)
  4. What is the element \(a_{23}\text{?}\)
  5. What is the element \(a_{32}\text{?}\)
Definition 2.3.3.

Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{ij} \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} b_{ij} \end{bmatrix}\) be two \(m\times n\) matrices. We define their sum by:

\begin{equation*} A + B = \begin{bmatrix} a_{ij} + b_{ij} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

That is, we add the corresponding entries together. The result is also an \(m\times n\) matrix.

Let \(A\) be an \(m\times k\) matrix, and let \(B\) be a \(k\times n\) matrix. Then the product \(AB = \begin{bmatrix}c_{ij} \end{bmatrix}\) is defined by:

\begin{equation*} c_{ij} = a_{i1}\cdot b_{1j} + a_{i2}\cdot b_{2j} + \dots + a_{ik}\cdot b_{kj} \end{equation*}

Definition 2.3.7.

The identity matrix of order \(n\) is the \(n \times n\) matrix:

\begin{equation*} I_n = \begin{bmatrix} \delta_{ij} \end{bmatrix} \text{ where } \delta_{ij} = \left\{ \begin{array}{lr} 1 & i = j \\ 0 & i \not= j \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

I'll refer to this as the \(n\times n\) identity matrix.

Note 2.3.8.

If \(A\) is an \(m \times n\) matrix, then:

\begin{equation*} A I_n = I_m A = A \end{equation*}
Definition 2.3.9.

If \(A\) is a square, \(n\times n\) matrix, we define \(A^r = A A A \dots A\text{,}\) the product of \(A\) with itself \(r\) times. Further, \(A^0 = I_n\text{.}\)

Example 2.3.10.

Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Find a formula for \(A^n, n\in \mathbb{Z}^+\)

Definition 2.3.11.

If \(A\) and \(B\) are square, \(n \times n\) matrices such that \({ AB = BA = I_n }\text{,}\) then \(B\) is called the inverse of \(A\text{,}\) and \(A\) is said to be invertible. We write \(B = A^{-1}\) to denote the inverse matrix.

Example 2.3.12.

  1. Using the formula in homework 2.3.3.2, find \(A^{-1}\)
  2. Find \(A^3\)
  3. Find \((A^{-1})^3\)
  4. Using the matrices above and matrix multiplication, show that \(A^3\) and \((A^{-1})^3\) are inverses.
Definition 2.3.13.

Let \(A\) be an \(m \times n\) matrix. We define the transpose of \(A\), denoted \(A^t\) to be the \(n\times m\) matrix obtained by interchanging the rows and columns of \(A\text{.}\)

Definition 2.3.14.

A square matrix is called symmetric if \(A = A^t\text{.}\)

Subsection 2.3.1 Binary Matrices and Operations

Definition 2.3.15.

A binary matrix (or Boolean matrix) is one for which all of its entries are either 0 or 1.

Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{ij} \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} b_{ij} \end{bmatrix}\) be two \(m\times n\) binary matrices. We define:

  • The join of \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) denoted \(A \lor B\text{,}\) to be the \(m \times n\) matrix whose \(ij\)th entry is \(a_{ij} \lor b_{ij}\text{.}\)
  • The meet of \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) denoted \(A \land B\text{,}\) to be the \(m \times n\) matrix whose \(ij\)th entry is \(a_{ij} \land b_{ij}\text{.}\)
Example 2.3.16.

If \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) find:

  1. Find the meet \(A \land B\)
  2. Find the join \(A \lor B\)
Definition 2.3.17.

Let \(A\) be an \(m\times k\) matrix, and let \(B\) be a \(k\times n\) matrix. Then the Boolean product \(A\odot B = \begin{bmatrix}c_{ij} \end{bmatrix}\) is defined by:

\begin{equation*} c_{ij} = ( a_{i1}\land b_{1j} ) \lor ( a_{i2}\land b_{2j} ) \lor \dots \lor ( a_{ik}\land b_{kj} ) \end{equation*}
Example 2.3.18.

If \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) find:

  1. \(A \odot B\)
  2. \(B \odot A\)
Definition 2.3.19.

If \(A\) is a square, \(n\times n\) binary matrix, we define \({ A^{[r]} = A\odot A\odot A\odot \dots \odot A }\text{,}\) the Boolean product of \(A\) with itself \(r\) times. Further, \(A^{[0]} = I_n\text{.}\)
